Electric Charges & Fields

God Master Notes. Concept Clarity Level: 100%.

Essential Constants:
• Charge of electron ($e$): $-1.6 \times 10^{-19} C$
• Mass of electron ($m_e$): $9.1 \times 10^{-31} kg$
• Mass of proton ($m_p$): $1.67 \times 10^{-27} kg$
• Permittivity of space ($\epsilon_0$): $8.854 \times 10^{-12} C^2/Nm^2$
• Coulomb Constant ($k$): $9 \times 10^9 Nm^2/C^2$

1. Electric Charge

DEFINITION Electric Charge is an intrinsic property of elementary particles (like mass) that causes them to experience a force when placed in an electromagnetic field.
Kid's Explanation Imagine every particle has a "personality".
  • Protons are "Positive" (+).
  • Electrons are "Negative" (-).
  • Neutrons are "Boring" (0).
Rule: Same personalities fight (Repel). Opposite personalities become best friends (Attract).

1.1 Basic Properties

  • Additivity: Charges add like real numbers ($Q = +5C - 2C = +3C$).
  • Conservation: Charge can neither be created nor destroyed. In $\beta$-decay: $n \to p + e^- + \bar{\nu}$ (Net charge 0 before & after).
  • Quantization ($q = ne$): Charge comes in packets. Smallest packet is $e$.
Solved Example: How many electrons in 1 Coulomb?

Q: Calculate the number of electrons constituting 1 Coulomb of charge.

Solution:

  • Formula: $q = ne \Rightarrow n = q/e$
  • Given: $q = 1C$, $e = 1.6 \times 10^{-19}C$
  • Calculation: $n = \frac{1}{1.6 \times 10^{-19}} = 6.25 \times 10^{18}$

Answer: $6.25 \times 10^{18}$ electrons.

2. Coulomb's Law

LAW Force between two point charges is proportional to product of charges and inversely proportional to square of distance.
$F = \frac{k |q_1 q_2|}{r^2}$

Logic: If distance ($r$) doubles, Force becomes $1/4$.
If charges double, Force becomes 4 times stronger.

3. Electric Field

DEFINITION Force experienced per unit positive test charge. A vector quantity.
Unit: $N/C$ or $V/m$.

Vector Form: $\vec{E} = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{q}{r^2} \hat{r}$

Deep Dive: Why "Test Charge" must be small?

We define $E = \lim_{q_0 \to 0} F/q_0$.

Why? If the test charge $q_0$ is big, its own electric field will push the main source charge ($Q$) away or disturb the setup. We want to measure the field without changing it. Imagine measuring the temperature of a coffee drop with a giant frozen thermometer—it would cool the drop! You need a tiny sensor.

4. Electric Dipole

Two charges $\pm q$ separated by $2a$.

  • Dipole Moment ($\vec{p}$): Vector pointing from Negative (-) to Positive (+).
  • Formula: $\vec{p} = q(2\vec{a})$

4.1 Electric Field due to Dipole

Derivation: Field on Axial Line

Goal: Find $E$ at distance $r$ from center on the axis.

  1. Field due to $+q$: $E_+ = \frac{kq}{(r-a)^2}$ (Away)
  2. Field due to $-q$: $E_- = \frac{kq}{(r+a)^2}$ (Towards)
  3. Net Field $E = E_+ - E_-$
  4. Solving: $E = kq [\frac{1}{(r-a)^2} - \frac{1}{(r+a)^2}]$
  5. Using $(r \gg a)$, we get:
$\vec{E}_{axial} = \frac{2k\vec{p}}{r^3}$

Direction: Same as dipole moment ($\vec{p}$).

Derivation: Field on Equatorial Line

Goal: Find $E$ at distance $r$ on perpendicular bisector.

  1. Magnitudes are equal: $E_+ = E_- = \frac{kq}{r^2+a^2}$.
  2. Vertical components ($E\sin\theta$) cancel out.
  3. Horizontal components ($E\cos\theta$) add up.
  4. $E_{net} = 2E\cos\theta$ (Opposite to $\vec{p}$).
$\vec{E}_{eq} = -\frac{k\vec{p}}{r^3}$

Direction: Opposite to dipole moment ($\vec{p}$). Note the negative sign!

4.2 Torque on Dipole ($\vec{\tau} = \vec{p} \times \vec{E}$)

Net Force is ZERO. Net Torque rotates it.

Angle ($\theta$) Torque ($\tau = pE\sin\theta$) Potential Energy ($U = -pE\cos\theta$) State
0° (Parallel) 0 (Min) $-pE$ (Min) Stable Equilibrium
90° (Perpendicular) $pE$ (Maximum) 0 Max Torque
180° (Anti-Parallel) 0 (Min) $+pE$ (Max) Unstable Equilibrium

5. Electric Flux & Gauss Law

5.1 Electric Flux ($\Phi_E$)

Measure of flow of field lines through an area.

Formula: $\Phi = \vec{E} \cdot \vec{A} = EA \cos\theta$

3 Cases of Flux:

  • $\theta = 0^\circ$ (Area $\perp$ Field): $\Phi = EA$ (Maximum Flux). Field pierces straight through.
  • $\theta = 90^\circ$ (Area || Field): $\Phi = 0$ (Zero Flux). Field skims the surface.
  • $\theta = 180^\circ$: $\Phi = -EA$ (Inward Flux).
Derivations: Gauss Law Applications

1. Long Wire ($\lambda$):
Surface: Cylinder. Flux only through curved part.
$E(2\pi rl) = q/\epsilon_0 \Rightarrow E = \frac{\lambda}{2\pi\epsilon_0 r}$.

2. Plane Sheet ($\sigma$):
Surface: Pillbox. Flux through 2 flat faces.
$2EA = \sigma A/\epsilon_0 \Rightarrow E = \frac{\sigma}{2\epsilon_0}$.
