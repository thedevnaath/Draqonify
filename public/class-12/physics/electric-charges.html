Electric Charges & Fields

The "God Master" Notes. Every concept, definition, and secret trick.

1. Electrostatics & Charge

DEFINITION: Electrostatics The branch of physics that deals with the study of forces, fields, and potentials arising from static charges (charges at rest).

1.1 Electric Charge

Charge is an intrinsic property of elementary particles (like mass) which gives rise to electric force.

  • Two Types: Positive (Proton) and Negative (Electron). Named by Benjamin Franklin.
  • Polarity: The property that differentiates the two kinds of charges.
  • Interaction: Like charges repel; Unlike charges attract.
Deep Dive: Gold Leaf Electroscope

A device used to detect the presence and polarity of charge.

  • Construction: A metal rod with a knob at top and two thin gold leaves at bottom, inside a glass jar.
  • Working: When a charged object touches the knob, charge flows down to the leaves. Since both leaves get the same charge, they repel and diverge.
  • Degree of Divergence: Measures the amount of charge.

1.2 Conductors vs Insulators

  • Conductors: Allow electricity to pass through them easily (e.g., Metals, Humans, Earth). They have free electrons.
  • Insulators: Do not allow passage of electricity (e.g., Glass, Plastic, Nylon).
  • Semiconductors: Intermediate behavior (Silicon, Germanium).
CONCEPT: Earthing (Grounding) The process of sharing the charges with the Earth. The Earth acts as a vast ocean of electrons—it can accept or provide infinite electrons to neutralize a body.

1.3 Methods of Charging

  1. By Friction: Rubbing two insulators. Electrons are physically torn off one and added to the other. (Glass + Silk → Glass becomes +, Silk becomes -).
  2. By Conduction: Direct contact between a charged body and a neutral conductor.
  3. By Induction: Charging without contact.
    1. Bring + rod near neutral sphere.
    2. Electrons gather near rod; + charges go to far side (Polarization).
    3. Ground the far side (+ charges flow to earth).
    4. Remove ground, then remove rod.
    5. Sphere remains negatively charged.

1.4 Basic Properties of Charge

  • Additivity: Total charge is the algebraic sum. ($Q = q_1 + q_2 - q_3$).
  • Conservation: Total charge of an isolated system remains constant. Example: In $\beta$-decay, neutron turns into proton + electron ($0 \to +e + (-e)$).
  • Quantization: Charge exists in discrete packets.
    q = ne where $n$ is an integer, $e = 1.6 \times 10^{-19} C$.

2. Coulomb's Law

LAW: Coulomb's Law The force between two point charges is directly proportional to the product of the magnitude of charges and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them.
F = k |q₁q₂| / r²

where $k = 1/4\pi\epsilon_0 = 9 \times 10^9 Nm^2/C^2$

2.1 Vector Form (Crucial for Direction)

$\vec{F}_{21} = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{q_1 q_2}{r^2} \hat{r}_{12}$

  • $\vec{F}_{21}$: Force on $q_2$ due to $q_1$.
  • $\hat{r}_{12}$: Unit vector from 1 to 2.
  • Significance: It proves Newton's 3rd Law ($\vec{F}_{12} = -\vec{F}_{21}$).

2.2 Dielectric Constant (Relative Permittivity)

When charges are placed in a medium (like water), the force decreases.

$\epsilon_r = K = \frac{F_{vacuum}}{F_{medium}} = \frac{\epsilon}{\epsilon_0}$

  • For Vacuum, $K=1$. For Air, $K \approx 1$.
  • For Water, $K=81$ (Force is 81 times weaker!).
  • For Metals, $K=\infty$ (Force vanishes).
JEE/NEET Rank Booster: Superposition

Principle: Force on a charge due to multiple charges is the Vector Sum of individual forces. Do NOT add them algebraically.

  • Null Point Shortcut: Two like charges $q_1, q_2$ separated by $L$. Null point is at $x$ from $q_1$ (smaller):
    x = L / (√(q₂/q₁) + 1)
  • Polygon Symmetry: If equal charges are at corners of a regular polygon (triangle, square...), net force on a center charge is ZERO.

3. Electric Field

DEFINITION: Electric Field The region around a charge where its electrical influence can be felt. Mathematically, Force per unit positive test charge.
Unit: N/C or V/m. Vector Quantity.

$\vec{E} = \lim_{q_0 \to 0} \frac{\vec{F}}{q_0}$

  • Point Charge: $E = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{Q}{r^2}$
  • Direction: Radially OUT for +ve, Radially IN for -ve.

3.1 Electric Field Lines (Properties)

  1. Start from Positive, End at Negative (or infinity).
  2. Never Cross: If they did, one point would have two directions of field (Impossible).
  3. Continuous Curves: No sudden breaks in charge-free regions.
  4. No Closed Loops: Electrostatic field is conservative.
  5. Crowded lines = Strong Field; Distant lines = Weak Field.

4. Electric Dipole

A system of two equal and opposite charges separated by a small distance $2a$.

  • Dipole Moment ($\vec{p}$): $\vec{p} = q \times 2a$.
  • Direction: From Negative to Positive.

4.1 Field of a Dipole (Derivations)

Position Formula Direction
Axial (End-on) $E = \frac{2kp}{r^3}$ Along $\vec{p}$
Equatorial (Broad-side) $E = \frac{kp}{r^3}$ Opposite to $\vec{p}$

Note: $E_{axial} = 2 E_{equatorial}$ for short dipoles.

4.2 Dipole in Uniform Field

  • Net Force: $F_{net} = +qE - qE = 0$ (Always).
  • Torque ($\tau$): $\vec{\tau} = \vec{p} \times \vec{E} = pE \sin\theta$.
  • Potential Energy (U): $U = -\vec{p} \cdot \vec{E} = -pE \cos\theta$.
Equilibrium States:
1. Stable ($\theta=0^\circ$): $U = -pE$ (Min). Torque = 0.
2. Unstable ($\theta=180^\circ$): $U = +pE$ (Max). Torque = 0.
3. Max Torque ($\theta=90^\circ$): $\tau = pE$.

5. Gauss's Law

5.1 Electric Flux ($\Phi$)

Total number of field lines crossing a surface normally.

$\Phi = \vec{E} \cdot \vec{A} = EA \cos\theta$

  • Area Vector ($\vec{A}$): Vector perpendicular to the surface. For a closed body (3D), it points Outward.
LAW: Gauss's Law The total electric flux through a closed surface is equal to $1/\epsilon_0$ times the net charge enclosed by the surface.
$\Phi = \oint \vec{E} \cdot d\vec{A} = \frac{q_{in}}{\epsilon_0}$

5.2 Applications (The Derivations)

A. Infinitely Long Straight Wire

  • Charge Density: Linear ($\lambda = q/l$).
  • Gaussian Surface: Cylinder of radius $r$, length $l$.
  • Result: $E = \frac{\lambda}{2\pi\epsilon_0 r}$
  • Graph: Hyperbola ($E \propto 1/r$).

B. Infinite Plane Sheet

  • Charge Density: Surface ($\sigma = q/A$).
  • Gaussian Surface: Pillbox (Cylinder piercing the sheet).
  • Result: $E = \frac{\sigma}{2\epsilon_0}$
  • Insight: $E$ is independent of distance! Uniform field.

C. Thin Spherical Shell

  • Charge Density: Surface ($\sigma$). Total Charge $q$.
  • Case 1: Outside ($r > R$): $E = \frac{kq}{r^2}$. (Behaves like point charge at center).
  • Case 2: Surface ($r = R$): $E = \frac{kq}{R^2}$ (Maximum).
  • Case 3: Inside ($r < R$): $E = 0$. (No enclosed charge).

6. Conductors in Electrostatic Fields

NCERT lists these crucial properties for a conductor in equilibrium:

  1. Inside, E = 0: Charges rearrange to cancel any external field.
  2. At Surface, E is Normal: If it had a tangential component, surface charges would move (current), violating static condition.
  3. Net Charge is on Surface: Like charges repel to the farthest boundary.
  4. Potential is Constant: Throughout the volume and surface.
  5. Shielding: A cavity inside a conductor has zero field, shielding it from outside influence.
