Electrostatic Potential and Capacitance

Scalar Side of Electricity. Energy, Work & Storage.

üìù The Big Picture (Read this first)

In Chapter 1, we studied the Vector side of electricity (Force and Field). Now, we study the Scalar side (Energy and Potential).
  • Analogy: If Electric Field is the "Slope" of a hill, Electric Potential is the "Height" of the hill.
  • Goal: To understand how much work it takes to assemble charges and how to store that energy in a device called a Capacitor.

2.1 Electrostatic Potential ($V$) üîπ

The Concept: The amount of work done to bring a unit positive charge from Infinity to a specific point against the electric force.
  • Formula:
    $$V = \frac{W}{q_0}$$
  • Unit: Volt ($V$) or Joule/Coulomb ($J/C$).
  • Scalar Quantity: It has magnitude but no direction. (Easier to add than Vectors!)
Conceptify Imagine a hill. The ground is "Zero Potential" (Infinity). Pushing a rock (Charge) up the hill is hard work. The higher you go, the more "Potential" the rock has. If you let go, it falls back down. Potential is just "Electric Height".

2.2 Potential due to a Point Charge (Derivation üõë)

Derivation: Potential at distance r

Goal: Calculate $V$ at distance $r$ from charge $+Q$.

  1. Setup: Charge $+Q$ at Origin. Bring test charge $+1C$ from $\infty$ to point $P$ (distance $r$).
  2. Work Done ($dW$): Force is repulsive ($F = kQ/x^2$). To move it closer by $dx$, we must apply equal opposite force.
    $$dW = \vec{F}_{ext} \cdot \vec{dx} = -F dx = - \frac{kQ}{x^2} dx$$
  3. Integrate: Total work $W = \int_{\infty}^{r} dW$.
    $$W = -kQ \int_{\infty}^{r} x^{-2} dx$$
    $$W = -kQ \left[ \frac{x^{-1}}{-1} \right]_{\infty}^{r} = kQ \left[ \frac{1}{r} - \frac{1}{\infty} \right]$$
  4. Result: Since $V = W/1C$:
    $$V = \frac{kQ}{r}$$

    Note: $V \propto 1/r$ (Potential falls slower than Field $E \propto 1/r^2$).

2.3 Potential due to an Electric Dipole (Derivation üõë)

Derivation: V due to Dipole

Setup: Dipole ($-q$ at A, $+q$ at B), separation $2a$. Find $V$ at point $P$ (distance $r$, angle $\theta$).

  1. Principle: $V_{net} = V_{+q} + V_{-q}$ (Scalar addition).
    $$V = \frac{kq}{r_1} - \frac{kq}{r_2} = kq \left( \frac{1}{r_1} - \frac{1}{r_2} \right)$$
  2. Geometry Approximation: If $r \gg a$:
    $r_1 \approx r - a \cos\theta$
    $r_2 \approx r + a \cos\theta$
  3. Substitute:
    $$V = kq \left[ \frac{(r + a \cos\theta) - (r - a \cos\theta)}{r^2 - a^2 \cos^2\theta} \right]$$
    $$V = kq \frac{2a \cos\theta}{r^2}$$
  4. Result: ($p = q \times 2a$)
    $$V = \frac{kp \cos\theta}{r^2}$$
CaseAngle ($\theta$)Potential
Axial$0^\circ$$kp/r^2$ (Max)
Equatorial$90^\circ$$0$ (Min)
Conceptify On the equator of a dipole, you are equally far from the "Good Twin" (+q) and the "Evil Twin" (-q). Their pulls cancel out perfectly. The height (Potential) is zero.

2.4 Equipotential Surfaces

The Concept: A surface where the potential is the same at every point.

Properties (Memorize these!):

  1. Work done to move a charge on this surface is Zero ($W = q\Delta V = 0$).
  2. Electric Field is always perpendicular to the surface.
  3. They never intersect.
  4. Shapes:
    • Point Charge: Concentric Spheres.
    • Uniform Field: Parallel Planes.

2.5 Potential Energy of a System ($U$)

The Concept: The energy stored in a system of charges because of their positions. It equals the work done to bring them together from infinity.
  • Formula (Two Charges):
    $$U = \frac{k q_1 q_2}{r_{12}}$$
    • Tip: If charges are same sign ($+,+$), $U > 0$ (Repulsive/Unstable). If opposite ($+,-$), $U < 0$ (Attractive/Stable).

Potential Energy of Dipole in External Field:

  • Work done to rotate dipole from $\theta_1$ to $\theta_2$ against Torque.
  • $$U = -pE \cos\theta = -\vec{p} \cdot \vec{E}$$
    • Stable ($\theta=0$): $U = -pE$ (Minimum Energy $\to$ Most Happy).
    • Unstable ($\theta=180$): $U = +pE$ (Max Energy $\to$ Angry).

2.6 Electrostatics of Conductors (The Golden Rules)

(Most Conceptual Questions come from here)

  1. Inside a conductor, Electrostatic Field is zero. (Shielding).
  2. At the surface, Field is normal (perpendicular).
  3. Interior is at constant Potential. ($V_{inside} = V_{surface}$).
  4. Charge resides only on the surface.
  5. Sharp Points: Surface Charge Density ($\sigma$) is higher at sharp points ($\sigma \propto 1/Radius$). Corona Discharge happens here.

2.7 Dielectrics and Polarization

  • Dielectric: A non-conducting material (Insulator) that gets polarized in an electric field.
  • Polarization: When placed in external field $E_0$, atoms stretch. Positive moves with field, Negative moves against. This creates a small internal field $E_{in}$.
  • Net Field: Decreases. $$E_{net} = E_0 - E_{in} = \frac{E_0}{K}$$
  • Dielectric Constant (K): $K = \frac{E_{applied}}{E_{reduced}}$.
Conceptify Imagine trying to shout through a pillow. The pillow (Dielectric) absorbs some of your sound (Field). The sound that comes out the other side is weaker. The thicker the pillow, the weaker the sound.

2.8 Capacitors and Capacitance ($C$) üîπ

The Concept: A device to store electric charge (and energy).
  • Formula:
    $$C = \frac{Q}{V}$$
  • Unit: Farad (F). (1F is huge, we usually use $\mu F$ or $pF$).
  • Meaning: "How much charge can you hold for every 1 Volt of push?"
Conceptify A Capacitor is a Bucket. Charge (Q) is the Water. Voltage (V) is the Water Pressure. Capacitance (C) is the width of the bucket. A wider bucket holds more water at the same pressure.

2.9 The Parallel Plate Capacitor (Derivation üõë)

Derivation: Capacitance Formula

Setup: Two plates of Area $A$, separation $d$, charge density $+\sigma$ and $-\sigma$.

  1. Electric Field between plates:
    $$E = \frac{\sigma}{\epsilon_0} = \frac{Q}{A\epsilon_0}$$
  2. Potential Difference ($V = Ed$):
    $$V = \left( \frac{Q}{A\epsilon_0} \right) d$$
  3. Capacitance ($C = Q/V$):
    $$C = \frac{Q}{(Qd / A\epsilon_0)}$$
  4. Result:
    $$C = \frac{\epsilon_0 A}{d}$$

    Insight: To increase C, make Area ($A$) big or Distance ($d$) small.

2.10 Effect of Dielectric on Capacitance

If you fill the space between plates with a dielectric (Constant $K$):

  • Field decreases ($E \to E/K$).
  • Voltage decreases ($V \to V/K$).
  • Capacitance Increases:
    $$C_{medium} = K C_{air}$$
    $$C = \frac{K \epsilon_0 A}{d}$$
Conceptify Putting a sponge (Dielectric) between the plates makes the "Springiness" of the field weaker. This allows you to stuff MORE charge in before the voltage gets too high. It's a cheat code to store more energy.

2.12 Energy Stored in a Capacitor ($U$)

The Concept: Work done to charge the capacitor is stored as Potential Energy.
  • Process: $dW = V \cdot dq = \frac{q}{C} dq$.
  • Integration: $\int_{0}^{Q} \frac{q}{C} dq = \frac{Q^2}{2C}$.
  • Three Formulas (Memorize all):
    1. $U = \frac{1}{2} C V^2$ (Use when V is constant).
    2. $U = \frac{Q^2}{2C}$ (Use when Q is constant/Series).
    3. $U = \frac{1}{2} Q V$.
  • Energy Density ($u$): Energy per unit volume in the electric field.
    $$u = \frac{1}{2} \epsilon_0 E^2$$

üß† "Never Forget This" - The Topper's Cheatsheet

  1. Work Done Relation: $W_{A \to B} = q(V_B - V_A)$.
    • Hint: If you move a proton towards a proton, Work is Positive (Hard). If you move electron towards proton, Work is Negative (It wants to go there).
  2. Battery Connected vs. Disconnected (The #1 Trick):
    • Battery Connected: $V$ stays constant. $Q$ changes ($Q=KCV$). $C$ becomes $KC$.
    • Battery Disconnected: $Q$ stays constant (Trapped). $V$ drops ($V/K$). $C$ becomes $KC$.
  3. Common Potential: When two capacitors ($C_1V_1, C_2V_2$) are connected:
    $$V_{common} = \frac{\text{Total Charge}}{\text{Total Capacitance}} = \frac{C_1V_1 + C_2V_2}{C_1 + C_2}$$
  4. Relationship between E and V:
    $$E = -\frac{dV}{dr}$$
    (Field flows from High Potential to Low Potential).

üìñ The "Remember Forever" Story

(The Logical Link: Height, Buckets, and Sponges)

1. Potential is Height:
Imagine a landscape. Positive Charge is a Mountain. Negative Charge is a Valley. Potential ($V$) is simply the Altitude.
‚Ä¢ To climb the mountain ($+ \to +$), you need energy (Work).
‚Ä¢ To fall into the valley ($+ \to -$), you gain energy.
‚Ä¢ The Electric Field is how steep the slope is. If the slope is steep ($E$), the height changes fast ($dV/dr$).

2. The Capacitor is a Bucket:
You want to store this "Height Energy". You build a tank with two walls (Plates).
‚Ä¢ Capacitance ($C$) is the width of the tank.
‚Ä¢ Charge ($Q$) is the water inside.
‚Ä¢ Voltage ($V$) is the water level.
‚Ä¢ Formula: $Q = CV$ (Water = Width $\times$ Level).

3. The Dielectric is a Sponge:
The tank walls are pushing away from each other (Repulsion). You stuff a Sponge (Dielectric) between them.
‚Ä¢ The Sponge absorbs the pressure (Field decreases).
‚Ä¢ This lets you pour in MORE water without the tank bursting.
‚Ä¢ Result: Capacity increases ($C \to KC$).

4. The Final Circuit:
‚Ä¢ Connecting tanks side-by-side (Parallel) = One giant wide tank ($C_1+C_2$).
‚Ä¢ Connecting tanks top-to-bottom (Series) = Harder to fill, total capacity drops.
